CFD Simulation of Synovial Fluid Flow in Hip Joint Replacements

Back to Home

Abstract

Artificial hip joint replacements have transformed orthopaedic care by significantly improving mobility and quality of life. However, challenges like wear particle generation can lead to tissue damage and implant failure. This project leverages Computational Fluid Dynamics (CFD) to model synovial fluid flow in a Tesla valve-integrated acetabular cup. The results demonstrate a novel approach to reducing wear particle accumulation, enhancing implant longevity, and mitigating complications like inflammation.

Methodology

The project utilized Fusion 360 for designing the acetabular cup with Tesla valve integration and ANSYS Fluent for simulating fluid dynamics. The Tesla valve's unidirectional flow properties were analyzed to mitigate backflow and wear particle transport. The methodology involved:

  • 3D modeling of the acetabular cup with Tesla valve integration.
  • Mesh generation with 0.2mm element size, resulting in 233,494 elements.
  • Simulation of synovial fluid flow dynamics, focusing on velocity, pressure, and density distribution.
  • Reversing inlet and outlet conditions to validate the Tesla valve's efficiency in controlling backflow.

Results

The CFD simulations revealed the following insights:

  • Velocity: High-velocity zones (up to 1.807 m/s) aligned with the Tesla valve's geometry, promoting optimal fluid transport and reducing wear particle residence time.
  • Pressure: Static pressure distributions highlighted the valve's effectiveness in impeding reverse flow.
  • Density: Uniform density confirmed incompressible flow behavior, mimicking biological synovial fluid.
  • Streamlines: Flow trajectories indicated reduced recirculation and stagnation, minimizing particle accumulation.

Discussion

The study demonstrated the Tesla valve's capability to direct fluid flow and restrict backflow, effectively managing wear particle transport in artificial hip joint replacements. High-pressure regions aligned with the valve's design to ensure unidirectional flow, while velocity and density contours validated the implant's performance under physiological conditions.

Challenges: The complex geometry of the Tesla valve posed computational challenges, and validation against experimental data is needed.

Future Work: Recommendations include exploring advanced materials for the acetabular cup, conducting dynamic simulations, and progressing to clinical trials.

Conclusion

This research highlights the potential of Tesla valve-integrated acetabular cups in enhancing the longevity and efficiency of hip joint replacements. By optimizing wear particle transport, the design addresses a critical limitation in current implants, paving the way for further advancements in biomedical engineering.
